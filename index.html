Consumer fintech · NextLeap capstone

Turning CRED's financial data into a behavioral mirror
RoleProduct strategy · solo
DomainPersonal finance (PFM), India
ContextNextLeap PM Fellowship capstone

Context

India's premium consumer-finance market is large and growing fast, and platforms like CRED capture transactions beautifully. But for multi-card, multi-account users, "what you spent" is everywhere and "why" is nowhere — the product exits the journey exactly where people need help making sense of their money.

The problem

Premium users hit financial cognitive overload. Personal-finance apps record and chart data but don't interpret it, so month-end summaries land as judgment — triggering guilt, then avoidance. Worse, raw spending charts mislead: peer-to-peer transfers and capital movements (say, a fixed deposit rolling into a mutual fund) pollute the picture, so even the numbers users see don't reflect real lifestyle spend.

Research & insight

Primary interviews surfaced two personas: a "Bill-only checker" who opens the app, sees a big number, feels uneasy, and closes it — and a "Curious Explorer" who digs in, finds subscription leaks, and feels back in control. The throughline: autopsy reports don't change behavior. Data shown after the fact, stripped of context, makes people defensive. The insight has to arrive earlier and feel safe.

What I proposed — a "behavioral mirror"

  • Financial noise cancellation — a pre-parsing layer that strips out transfers and capital movements to isolate true discretionary spend before anything is shown
  • Point-of-intent nudges — a neutral prompt at the pre-payment screen ("you've spent ₹240 more here than last month"), so awareness arrives at the moment of agency, not as regret after
  • Narrative spending insights — plain-language stories ("dining spikes the second weekend, just after payday — 4 of the last 6 months") instead of pie charts
  • Trust by design — every insight is a soft hypothesis with one-tap Yes / No / Edit, confidence levels, opt-in controls, and the option to exclude sensitive categories — directly answering surveillance anxiety and misclassification guilt

How I'd measure success

75%target read-rate
23%industry baseline
100-user pilot

Primary metric: the open-and-read rate of monthly reflection cards (targeting ~75% against a ~23% baseline). Alongside it, a trust metric — the share of AI narratives users confirm as accurate — and retention velocity for multi-card cohorts after heavy-spend cycles, validated through a 100-user pilot.

What I'd take forward

In consumer finance the unlock isn't more data or another chart — it's interpretation delivered with emotional safety. Designing for trust (uncertainty framing, reversibility, real user agency) mattered as much as the underlying model.
